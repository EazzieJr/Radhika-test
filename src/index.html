The fawn

A random fact about a fawn relating to the current action

First Steps

Within just 30 minutes of birth, a fawn is already finding its footing. By their second day, they can walk gracefully enough to follow their mother through dense brush.

Defying Gravity

This "bouncy" jumping is sometimes called stotting or pronking. It’s a signal to predators that the fawn is healthy and energetic—basically saying, "Don't bother chasing me, I'm too fast!"

Natural Mariners

Deer are surprisingly excellent swimmers. Their hair is hollow, which acts like a built-in life jacket by providing extra buoyancy while they navigate streams or lakes.

The Art of Hiding

When a fawn sits, it is often practicing "bedding down." They are coached by their mothers to stay perfectly still to avoid the gaze of passing predators.

Built for Independence

Fawns are "precocial" animals, meaning they are born in an advanced state. Standing up quickly is a survival evolution to ensure they don't remain vulnerable on the forest floor for too long.

High-Speed Agility

Even at a young age, fawns can reach speeds of up to 30 miles per hour. They often run in a zigzag pattern to confuse anything chasing them.

Invisible & Scentless

For the first few weeks of life, fawns have virtually no body scent. By lying still in tall grass, they become "invisible" to the noses of wolves and coyotes.

Spirited Defense

While they prefer to hide, a cornered fawn will use its front hooves to strike. This "flailing" motion is surprisingly sharp and serves as a last-resort defense mechanism.

Always Alert

Even when "doing nothing," a fawn's ears are constantly rotating independently. They can track sounds from different directions without even moving their heads.
